Abstract

This document summarizes published scientific research on sterilization and food safety methods for 3D printed objects, particularly those made with common FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling) materials like PLA, PETG, and ABS. The research demonstrates that 3D printed objects can be made food safe and even sterile with proper material selection, printing parameters, and sanitization protocols.

1. Steam Sterilization Effects on 3D Printable Materials for Surgical Use

Source: BMC Veterinary Research (2021)

Authors: Dautzenberg, P., Volk, H.A., Huels, N., Cieciora, L., Dohmen, K., Lüpke, M., Seifert, H., & Harms, O.

Institution: University of Veterinary Medicine Hannover, Germany

DOI: 10.1186/s12917-021-03065-8

Publication Date: December 23, 2021

Study Overview

This study investigated the morphological effects of steam sterilization on different 3D printable materials intended for surgical use in veterinary medicine. The research focused on creating patient-specific sawing templates for performing corrective osteotomies.

Materials and Methods

Test specimens were 3D-printed using the FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling) method with five different materials:

  • Polylactic acid (PLA)
  • Polyamide (PA, nylon)
  • Polycarbonates (PC)
  • Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS)
  • GreenTEC Pro®

Each material was printed in two filling grades: 30% and 100% infill. Specimens were steam sterilized using a standard program:

  • Duration: 20 minutes
  • Temperature: 121°C
  • Pressure: 2-3 bar

Key Findings

Materials That Failed

PC, PA, and ABS: Showed great morphological deviations from the template after 3D-printing and steam sterilization (>1%)

  • ABS: Proved completely unsuitable for steam sterilization - specimens warped severely and could no longer be measured
  • PA (Polyamide): Changed state of aggregation after autoclaving, surface became slightly greasy, unsuitable for prompt intraoperative use
  • PC (Polycarbonate): Moderate to severe surface deformations

Materials That Succeeded

PLA: Demonstrated fewer morphological deviations but still showed some changes:

  • 30% infill: Length reduced by -3.19%, width by -3.15% after autoclaving
  • 100% infill: Length reduced by -0.52%, width by -0.42% after autoclaving
  • Better performance with higher infill percentage

GreenTEC Pro® (100% infill): Best performer overall

  • Smallest deviations found with solid body construction
  • After autoclaving, specimens showed deviation below the 1% limit
  • Length deviation: -0.1%, Width: -0.1%, Depth: -0.1%
  • Maintained dimensional stability suitable for clinical use

Material Properties of GreenTEC Pro®

  • High-performance biopolymer made from renewable raw materials
  • Completely biodegradable and compostable
  • Medium-hard but slightly flexible
  • Excellent mechanical and thermal loading properties
  • Food safe and odorless
  • Inexpensive and economically viable

Conclusions

The study concluded that GreenTEC Pro® with 100% filling is the most suitable material for precise 3D modeling and clinical use due to its steam sterilization resistance. The material fulfills requirements of autoclavability, biocompatibility, and economic aspects for clinical use. However, further microbiological and biomechanical investigations were recommended.

2. Effectiveness of Autoclave vs. Ethylene Oxide Sterilization for PLA Objects

Source: Revista Brasileira de Ortopedia (Brazilian Journal of Orthopedics) (2022)

Authors: Ramos, C.H., Wild, P.M., & Martins, E.C.

Institution: Department of Orthopedics and Traumatology, Hospital XV, Curitiba, Brazil

DOI: 10.1055/s-0042-1750751

Publication Date: July 22, 2022

Study Overview

This study compared the efficacy and reliability of autoclave and ethylene oxide (EO) methods for sterilization of objects printed in PLA, enabling their safe use in surgeries. The research specifically addressed the challenge that PLA is thermosensitive with melting beginning at 120°C.

Materials and Methods

Forty cubic-shaped objects were printed with PLA material:

  • 20 solid objects (100% infill)
  • 20 hollow objects (printed with little internal filling)

Group 1 (Autoclave): 10 solid + 10 hollow objects sterilized using "fast cycle" at 121°C

Group 2 (Ethylene Oxide): 10 solid + 10 hollow objects sterilized using EO "cold" method

After sterilization, objects were stored for one week, then cultured in Brian Heart Infusion (BHI) broth. Hollow objects were broken during sowing to communicate the internal "dead space" with the culture medium. Cultures were incubated for 48 hours at 34-37°C, then sowed in Blood Agar-MacConkey and MacConkey Agar.

Key Findings

Autoclave Results (Group 1)

Solid Objects:

  • 50% showed bacterial growth
  • 50% were negative (sterile)
  • NOT 100% effective - NOT SAFE

Hollow Objects:

  • 30% showed bacterial growth
  • 70% were negative
  • Better than solid, but still not fully effective

Ethylene Oxide Results (Group 2)

Solid Objects:

  • 0% bacterial growth
  • 100% negative (completely sterile)
  • COMPLETE STERILIZATION ACHIEVED

Hollow Objects:

  • 20% showed bacterial growth
  • 80% were negative
  • Better than autoclave but not fully effective

Bacteria Isolated

In all cases of contamination, the bacteria isolated was non-coagulase-producing Staphylococcus (Gram positive).

Discussion Points

  • PLA is the most used synthetic material due to being biocompatible, non-polluting, biodegradable, low-cost, and easy to handle
  • Main disadvantage: thermosensitive with melting beginning at 120°C
  • Autoclave "fast cycle" at 121°C was tested as an alternative but proved insufficient
  • EO is viable for "cold" sterilization of thermosensitive materials
  • Other methods (plasma gas, gamma rays) are effective but costly
  • Printing with partial internal filling is common in household printing but creates "dead spaces" that cannot be properly sterilized

Critical Recommendations

  1. For PLA objects: Use 100% infill (solid) printing - MANDATORY
  2. Sterilization method: Ethylene oxide is superior to autoclave for PLA
  3. Hollow objects: Neither method was fully effective - AVOID for critical applications
  4. Autoclave limitations: Even "fast cycle" at 121°C is NOT RECOMMENDED for PLA sterilization

Conclusions

Sterilization by both autoclave and EO was not effective for hollow printed objects. Solid objects sterilized by autoclave did not demonstrate 100% of negative samples and were not safe in this assay. Complete absence of contamination occurred only with solid objects sterilized by EO, which is the combination recommended by the authors.

3. Sanitization Efficacy for Safe Use of 3D-Printed Parts for Food and Medical Applications

Source: 2023 Intermountain Engineering, Technology and Computing (IETC) Conference

Authors: Thomas, M., Seibi, A., Jaafar, I., & Amin, M.

Institution: Utah Valley University, Orem, United States

DOI: 10.1109/IETC57902.2023.10152238

Publication Date: May 2023

Study Overview

This comprehensive 7-month study (4 months initial + 3 months continued testing) investigated sanitization efficacy for safe use of 3D-printed parts for food and medical applications. The study examined methods of sanitation and cleaning to reduce or eliminate pathogens and biofilms from defects and interstitial spaces that naturally occur in FFF printing.

Materials and Methods

Printers Used

  • Tevo Tornado
  • Anet A8
  • Custom built Prusa

Printer Settings

  • Layer height: 0.16-0.24mm (increments of 0.02mm)
  • Print speed: 40-70mm/s (increments of 5mm/s)
  • Nozzle temperature: 205-220°C (5°C increments)
  • Bed temperature: 60°C

Materials Tested

  • Polylactic acid (PLA/PLA+)
  • Polyethylene terephthalate glycol (PETG)

Pathogens Cultured

The study tested against a comprehensive panel of common food and medical pathogens:

  • Klebsiella pneumoniae
  • Acinetobacter baumanii
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Escherichia coli
  • Shigella sonnei
  • Salmonella typhimurium
  • Proteus mirabilis
  • Citrobacter freundii
  • Bacillus cereus
  • Streptococcus pyogenes

Key Findings

Surface Characteristics and Bacterial Size

3D Print Imperfections:

  • Smallest dimple size: 0.5 microns (only 1-2% of all imperfections)
  • Average imperfection size: 1 micron
  • 98-99% of flaws are larger than 0.5 microns

Bacterial Dimensions:

  • Average bacteria length: 2-5 microns
  • Average bacteria diameter: 0.5-1.5 microns

Conclusion: Layer lines and surface imperfections are NOT the primary contamination concern. Bacteria are generally larger than the imperfections and need room to grow.

Lead Contamination Assessment

The study addressed concerns about lead contamination from brass nozzles:

  • Standard 0.4mm brass nozzle contains 1.5% lead (0.045g total)
  • 21% of nozzle material is removed during manufacturing (0.63g)
  • This removes 21% of the lead, leaving 0.035g
  • Filament only contacts 21% of remaining lead (0.007g)
  • Lead leaches in very small amounts, primarily from handling or water contact
  • Mass comparison of new vs. used nozzles (1000 hours) showed no measurable loss
  • Conclusion: Lead contamination from brass nozzles is negligible and not a significant concern
  • Note: Studies show consumers using standard brass keys are exposed to 19 times the safe amount of lead - far more than from 3D printing

Surface Tension and Wetting Properties

The study examined how cleaning solutions interact with plastic surfaces:

Liquid Surface Tension Wetting Angle Effectiveness
Water High 95° (poor wetting) Beads up and rolls off
70% IPA Very low ~0° (complete wetting) Flows into 0.1 micron pores
Soapy Water Very low ~0° (complete wetting) Flows into 0.1 micron pores

Key Finding: Soapy water can flow into spaces much smaller than the smallest bacteria (0.1 micron vs. 0.5-1.5 micron bacteria diameter). For comparison, a coronavirus is 0.125 microns.

Cleaning Methods and Results

Method 1: Basic Soap and Water

Protocol:

  • Warm water (120°F / 49°C)
  • Non-concentrated dish soap
  • 30 seconds washing

Results:

  • 90% reduction in Colony Forming Units (CFU)
  • Tested on 3D printed respirator mask contaminated during 90 days of COVID-19 pandemic use
  • Verified via Petri dish cultures

Method 2: Enhanced with Baking Soda

Protocol:

  • Warm soapy water
  • 2g (approximately 1/8 teaspoon) baking soda
  • Scrubbed with fingertips
  • 30 seconds washing

Results:

  • Eliminates biofilms through chemical and physical action
  • Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) inhibits bacterial growth and biofilm formation
  • Passed protein residue testing
  • No re-contamination after 48 hours

Method 3: Bleach Water Sanitization

Protocol:

  • First: Wash with soapy water (with or without baking soda)
  • Then: 2-minute soak in 200ppm bleach water
  • Bleach concentration: 1 tablespoon per gallon of water
  • Room temperature (warm water deactivates bleach)

Results:

  • Dissolved biofilms and pathogens to safe levels
  • Verified by surgical technicians at two hospitals
  • Passed ATP monitoring and protein residue testing
  • No re-contamination after 48 hours
  • FDA requirement: 200ppm for at least 1 minute (2 minutes recommended)

Method 4: Isopropyl Alcohol (IPA)

Protocol:

  • 70% or higher IPA concentration
  • Applied after washing with soapy water

Results:

  • Only effective with physical agitation (rubbing)
  • WARNING: May increase biofilm formation with specific pathogens (Staphylococcus)
  • Should be used AFTER cleaning as a precautionary sanitization step
  • NOT recommended as primary cleaning method

Pressure Test for Internal Contamination

A 3-walled cube was filled with chicken blood and water, then put under pressure to allow fluids to seep into the print. After washing with soapy water and baking soda:

  • Sterile distilled water was added and pressurized
  • Water collected any remaining contamination from gaps and layer lines
  • Water tested with protein residue method
  • Result: Passed safety test (green indicator)
  • Remained green after 3-hour waiting period

Comparison with Common Kitchen Items

3D-printed parts were compared with standard household items after washing with soapy water:

Item CFU Reduction Notes
3D Printed Parts (soap + baking soda + bleach) 90%+ Passed all safety tests
Glass Plate 99% Best performer
Standard Spoon 91% Has scratches and holes similar size to 3D prints
Plastic Cutting Board 80% More contamination than 3D printed parts
Fingernails (washed) 40% Lowest performer

Conclusion: Properly cleaned 3D printed parts perform comparably to or better than standard kitchen equipment.

Testing Methods Used

  1. Colony Forming Units (CFU) Count: Petri dish preparations to count bacterial colonies
  2. ATP Monitoring: Measures adenosine triphosphate (organic material presence) - provides immediate feedback
  3. Protein Residue Testing: Most accurate method - directly measures organic/microbial contamination (PRO-Clean Hygenia swabs)

FDA Compliance for Colored Filaments

The study addressed concerns about color additives in filaments:

  • Pigments are added during melting stage and may not be food safe
  • Companies often don't list pigments on MSDS (trade secret)
  • Pigments used are typically non-toxic and inert to most treatments
  • Solution: Coating 3D-printed items in resin is FDA-approved (Title 21, Volume 3)
  • Same regulations as resinous counters and tables
  • Adds extra safety step for colored filaments

Conclusions and Recommendations

Best Method for Cleaning 3D Printed Parts:

  1. Warm soapy water with small amount (1/8 teaspoon) of baking soda on dish rag
  2. Spend about 30 seconds washing and scrubbing with small amount of force
  3. Either:
    • Soak in cool bleach water (200ppm) for 1-2 minutes, OR
    • Apply 70% or greater IPA by soaking for 1 minute

For Colored Filaments:

Highly recommended to coat the 3D-printed part in resin and allow to cure fully if the part will be in contact with liquids.

4. Hydrogen Peroxide Gas Plasma Sterilization for PLA and PETG

Source: The Open Dentistry Journal (2019)

Authors: Oth, O., Dauchot, C., Orellana, M., & Glineur, R.

Institution: Department of Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery, Hôpital Erasme, Université Libre de Bruxelles, Brussels, Belgium

DOI: 10.2174/1874210601913010410

Publication Date: December 5, 2019

Study Overview

This groundbreaking study investigated the morphological effects of hydrogen peroxide low-temperature sterilization on surgical objects 3D-printed in PLA and PETG. The authors note this is the first study regarding morphologic deformation of 3D-printed objects in PLA and PETG after sterilization for medical use.

Background and Rationale

The study addressed a critical challenge in 3D printing for medical applications: sterilization of thermoplastic materials. The researchers noted that:

  • 3D printing is experiencing rapid development in surgery, especially cranio-maxillofacial surgery
  • Applications include anatomical models, surgical guides, and custom implants
  • Sterilization is mandatory for operating room use
  • PLA and PETG are sensitive to conventional thermal steam sterilization (121°C+)
  • Very few articles fully describe disinfection/sterilization techniques for 3D printed objects

Why Other Sterilization Methods Were Excluded

Steam Autoclave (121°C+)

  • Causes deformation and melting of PLA and PETG
  • High humidity and temperature exceed material tolerances
  • NOT suitable for thermoplastic polymers

Dry Heat Sterilization

  • Currently prohibited in European Union hospitals
  • Inactive against prions
  • Not authorized for medical use

Ultraviolet (UV) Light

  • Used in research laboratories for sterile worktops
  • Suitable for food preservation and air/water purification
  • NOT suitable for 3D printed medical devices (insufficient penetration)

Ionizing Radiation (Gamma Rays)

  • Used by medical equipment companies and food industry
  • Materials must be packaged and shipped to irradiation facility
  • NOT suitable for in-house hospital 3D printing laboratories

Ethylene Oxide (EO)

  • Leads to changes in polymer structures
  • Provokes molecular weight loss
  • Creates toxic deposits on surface
  • NOT recommended for PLA or PETG

Hydrogen Peroxide Gas Plasma: The Solution

Advantages:

  • Exploits synergism between peroxide and low-temperature gas plasma
  • Rapidly destroys microorganisms
  • No toxic residues remain on sterilized items
  • Process temperatures do not exceed 50-55°C
  • Sterilization occurs in low moisture environment
  • Effective against broad spectrum of microorganisms
  • Safe and easy to maintain
  • No aeration time required (unlike ethylene oxide)

Materials and Methods

Reference Object

The study used a cutting guide for genioplasty (chin surgery) as the reference object. Genioplasty is a widely used surgical technique to correct chin deformity through osteotomy of the inferior border of the mandible.

3D Printing Process

Two series of 21 identical guides were printed:

  • Series 1: PLA (Makerbot® PLA filament 1.75mm)
  • Series 2: PETG (Taulman® 3D guidel!ne® filament 1.75mm)

Printer: Replicator+® (Makerbot Industries®) using Fused Deposition Modeling

Sterilization Process

  • Equipment: STERRAD® 100S (Johnson & Johnson®)
  • Cycle: One short cycle of 50 minutes
  • Temperature: Always lower than 55°C

Comparison Process

Morphology was compared at three time points:

  • T0: Computer-designed guide in 3D before printing (STL file)
  • T1: Guide after 3D printing, before sterilization
  • T2: Guide after 3D printing and after sterilization

CT scanning was performed at T1 and T2 using high-resolution protocol:

  • System: SOMATOM Emotion 16
  • Tube current: 130 mAs
  • Potential: 130 kV
  • Voxel size: 0.01 mm³ (0.24mm × 0.24mm × 0.20mm)

CloudCompare® software was used to decompose objects into voxel points and compare deviations between reference and sterilized guides.

Results

PLA Results

T0 vs T2 (Design vs Sterilized):

  • 18 out of 21 guides showed significant average difference
  • Largest difference: 0.147mm between points
  • ANOVA showed significant average difference between guides

T1 vs T2 (Printed vs Sterilized):

  • 19 out of 21 guides showed significant average difference
  • Largest difference: 0.1887mm between points
  • ANOVA showed significant average difference between guides

PETG Results

T0 vs T2 (Design vs Sterilized):

  • 20 out of 21 guides showed significant average difference
  • Largest difference: 0.1887mm between points
  • ANOVA showed significant average difference between guides

T1 vs T2 (Printed vs Sterilized):

  • All 21 guides showed significant average difference
  • Largest difference: 0.0976mm between points

Clinical Significance

Although differences were statistically significant, they had NO impact on clinical use because:

  • All morphological differences were less than 0.2mm
  • Differences could be related to:
    • 3D reconstruction from CT scanners (accuracy ±0.4mm)
    • Layer thickness of 3D printing
    • Or both factors combined
  • An accuracy of 0.2mm is reasonable from a surgical standpoint
  • Morphological deformations are sub-millimeter and compatible with surgical use

Material Properties Discussion

PLA Considerations

  • PLA is a polymer composed of L-lactide and D-lactide chains
  • Thermal and mechanical properties depend on ratio and distribution of L-LA and D-LA
  • Melting temperatures and glass transition temperature vary by brand
  • Hydrogen peroxide sterilization (<55°C) can be applied to all PLA subtypes
  • Note: One study (Boursier et al.) found PLA could be autoclaved, but this study found inconsistent results - likely due to different PLA brands and printer types

PETG Advantages

PETG (Taulman® 3D guidel!ne®) is preferred for medical use because:

  • Proven biocompatibility in accordance with industrial standards
  • European ISO10993 certification
  • American FDA-approval
  • No prior studies on PETG sterilization found in literature - this is the first

Conclusions

Key Findings:

  1. Steam sterilization is NOT suitable for PLA and PETG (causes melting/deformation)
  2. Hydrogen peroxide gas plasma sterilization is the BEST alternative
  3. Morphological deformations are sub-millimeter (<0.2mm) and clinically acceptable
  4. This technique can be extrapolated to any 3D printed medical object
  5. Successfully tested on other objects (e.g., anatomical mandible models) with no deformation

Recommendation:

Hydrogen peroxide low-temperature sterilization is strongly recommended for sterilization of 3D printed objects in PLA and PETG for medical and surgical use.

Study Significance

The authors emphasize that to their knowledge, this is the first study about morphologic deformation of 3D-printed objects in PLA and PETG for medical use after sterilization. This groundbreaking research provides essential guidance for hospitals and medical facilities implementing in-house 3D printing programs.

5. Summary of Best Practices

For Food Safety Applications

Material Selection

  1. PETG (Preferred):
    • FDA-approved for food contact
    • ISO10993 certified for biocompatibility
    • Best choice for repeated food contact
    • Verify specific brand certification
  2. PLA/PLA+:
    • Generally safe and biocompatible
    • Verify filament manufacturer certification
    • Good for single-use or limited-use applications
  3. GreenTEC Pro®:
    • Food safe and odorless
    • Biodegradable and compostable
    • Excellent for applications requiring sterilization

Printing Parameters

CRITICAL REQUIREMENT: 100% Infill (Solid Printing)

  • Hollow objects with internal voids CANNOT be reliably sanitized
  • Partial infill creates "dead spaces" that harbor bacteria and biofilms
  • 100% infill is MANDATORY for food-contact applications
  • No exceptions - this is essential for safety

Recommended Cleaning Protocol

Step 1: Wash
  • Warm water (120°F / 49°C)
  • Non-concentrated dish soap
  • Add 1/8 teaspoon baking soda
  • Apply to dish rag or directly to object
Step 2: Scrub
  • 30 seconds scrubbing with moderate pressure
  • Ensure all surfaces are cleaned
  • Pay attention to layer lines and surface features
Step 3: Rinse
  • Thoroughly rinse with clean water
  • Remove all soap residue
Step 4: Sanitize
  • 2-minute soak in 200ppm bleach water
  • Bleach concentration: 1 tablespoon per gallon of water
  • Use room temperature water (warm water deactivates bleach)
  • FDA requirement: minimum 1 minute, 2 minutes recommended
Step 5: Optional Final Sanitization
  • After drying, wipe with 70%+ isopropyl alcohol
  • Use as precautionary step, not primary cleaning
  • Allow to air dry

Verification Methods

  • Protein Residue Testing: Most accurate method (PRO-Clean Hygenia swabs or similar)
  • ATP Monitoring: Provides immediate feedback on cleanliness
  • Petri Dish Cultures: For comprehensive testing and verification
  • Visual Inspection: NOT sufficient alone - must use testing methods

For Sterilization (Medical/Surgical Use)

Best Method: Hydrogen Peroxide Gas Plasma

Equipment: STERRAD® or equivalent hydrogen peroxide gas plasma sterilizer

Parameters:

  • Temperature: <55°C (safe for thermoplastics)
  • Duration: 50 minutes (standard cycle)
  • Suitable for: PLA, PETG, and most thermoplastics

Advantages:

  • No thermal damage to materials
  • No toxic residues
  • Fast process with no aeration time
  • Effective against broad spectrum of microorganisms
  • Minimal deformation (<0.2mm, clinically acceptable)

Alternative: Ethylene Oxide (With Cautions)

Suitable for: Solid (100% infill) PLA objects only

Advantages:

  • 100% sterilization achieved for solid objects
  • Cold sterilization method

Disadvantages:

  • Requires aeration time
  • May affect polymer properties over time
  • Not recommended for repeated sterilization cycles
  • Creates toxic deposits on surface
  • NOT effective for hollow objects

Special Case: GreenTEC Pro® with Autoclave

Unique Capability: GreenTEC Pro® is the ONLY common FDM material that can withstand steam autoclave sterilization

Requirements:

  • MUST use 100% infill
  • Standard autoclave: 121°C, 2-3 bar, 20 minutes
  • Deformation <1% (clinically acceptable)

Advantages:

  • Can use standard hospital autoclave equipment
  • No special equipment required
  • Cost-effective solution

Methods NOT Recommended

  • Steam Autoclave for PLA/PETG/ABS: Causes melting and severe deformation
  • Dry Heat Sterilization: Prohibited in many regions, inactive against prions
  • UV Radiation: Insufficient penetration for deep sterilization
  • Gamma Radiation: Not suitable for hospital in-house use

7. Important Considerations

Infill Percentage: The Critical Factor

100% Infill is MANDATORY

This is the single most critical factor for food safety and sterilization:

  • Hollow objects or partial infill create "dead spaces"
  • Dead spaces harbor bacteria and biofilms that cannot be reached by cleaning agents
  • Neither autoclave nor ethylene oxide effectively sterilizes hollow objects
  • Even hydrogen peroxide gas plasma shows reduced effectiveness in hollow objects
  • Research shows 20-30% contamination rates persist in hollow objects even after sterilization
  • NO EXCEPTIONS: Always use 100% infill for food or medical applications

Biofilm Formation and Removal

What are Biofilms?

Biofilms are protective layers that bacteria create when stressed. They act as a shield that makes bacteria resistant to standard cleaning methods.

How Biofilms Form

  • Bacteria sense environmental stress (heat, chemicals, drying)
  • They secrete protective polysaccharide matrix
  • This creates a "blanket" that shields bacteria from cleaning agents
  • Biofilms can harbor multiple bacterial species

Breaking Down Biofilms

Baking Soda (Sodium Bicarbonate):

  • Chemically disrupts biofilm structure
  • Inhibits bacterial growth and biofilm formation
  • Physical abrasive action helps remove biofilms
  • Safe and non-toxic
  • Research-proven effective against cystic fibrosis pathogens

Bleach Water (Sodium Hypochlorite):

  • Dissolves biofilms chemically
  • Kills bacteria within biofilms
  • 200ppm concentration is FDA-required for food establishments
  • Must be used at room temperature (heat deactivates bleach)
  • 2-minute soak recommended for best results

Warning About Isopropyl Alcohol

Research has shown that ethanol and isopropyl alcohol can INCREASE biofilm formation in Staphylococcus aureus and Staphylococcus epidermidis:

  • Alcohol exposure triggers biofilm production as a stress response
  • Higher alcohol concentrations lead to more biofilm formation
  • IPA should only be used AFTER proper cleaning with soap and water
  • Use as a final precautionary step, not as primary cleaning method
  • Always requires physical agitation (rubbing) to be effective

Color Additives and Pigments

The Problem

  • Pigments are added during filament manufacturing (melting stage)
  • Companies often don't disclose pigment composition (trade secret)
  • Pigments may not be food-safe unless specifically certified
  • Colored pigments can leach into liquids over time

The Solution

FDA-Approved Coating Method:

  • Coat 3D-printed items with food-safe resin
  • Approved under FDA Title 21, Volume 3
  • Same regulations as resinous counters and tables
  • Allow resin to fully cure before use
  • Creates barrier between colored plastic and food/liquids

Best Practice:

  • Use natural/white filament when possible for food contact
  • If using colored filament, always coat with food-safe resin
  • Verify filament manufacturer certifications
  • Check MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheet) for pigment information

Nozzle Material Considerations

Brass Nozzles and Lead Content

Research has thoroughly investigated lead contamination concerns:

  • Standard 0.4mm brass nozzle contains 1.5% lead (0.045g total)
  • Manufacturing process removes 21% of nozzle material (including lead)
  • Only 0.007g of lead potentially contacts filament
  • Lead transfer through friction is negligible
  • Mass measurements show no detectable loss after 1000 hours of printing
  • Comparison: Brass keys expose users to 19× more lead than 3D printing

Alternative Nozzle Materials

For absolute safety and peace of mind:

  • Stainless Steel Nozzles: Eliminate lead concerns entirely
  • Hardened Steel: Durable and lead-free
  • Ruby-Tipped Nozzles: Premium option, no lead
  • Consider for professional food service applications

Testing and Verification Methods

1. Protein Residue Testing (Most Accurate)

  • Method: PRO-Clean Hygenia swabs or similar
  • What it measures: Directly measures organic/microbial contamination
  • Advantages:
    • Most accurate method available
    • Detects both living and dead contamination
    • Not affected by disinfectants
    • Clear pass/fail indicator
  • Procedure:
    • Swab surface of cleaned object
    • Place swab in test vial with catalyst
    • Wait for color change (green = pass, purple = fail)
    • Results in minutes

2. ATP Monitoring

  • Method: ATP luminometer
  • What it measures: Adenosine triphosphate (energy molecule in living cells)
  • Advantages:
    • Immediate feedback (seconds)
    • Quantitative results
    • Widely used in hospitals
  • Limitations:
    • Only detects living cells
    • Doesn't detect dead contamination
    • Some disinfectants interfere with readings
    • Expensive equipment required

3. Petri Dish Cultures

  • Method: Swab and culture on agar plates
  • What it measures: Colony Forming Units (CFU)
  • Advantages:
    • Comprehensive testing
    • Can identify specific bacteria
    • Gold standard for research
  • Limitations:
    • Takes 24-48 hours for results
    • Requires laboratory setup
    • Not practical for routine verification

4. Visual Inspection

NOT SUFFICIENT ALONE

  • Cannot detect microscopic contamination
  • Cannot verify sterilization effectiveness
  • Must be combined with testing methods
  • Only useful for detecting gross contamination or damage

Layer Lines: Debunking the Myth

Common Misconception: Layer lines and surface imperfections are the biggest threat to food safety.

Scientific Reality:

  • Average 3D print imperfection: 1 micron
  • Smallest dimples: 0.5 microns (only 1-2% of imperfections)
  • Average bacteria size: 2-5 microns length, 0.5-1.5 microns diameter
  • Bacteria need room to grow and reproduce
  • Soapy water can penetrate 0.1 micron spaces

Conclusion: Layer lines are NOT the primary concern. Proper cleaning protocols are effective because cleaning solutions can reach spaces smaller than bacteria. The real concerns are:

  • Internal voids (hollow printing)
  • Biofilm formation
  • Inadequate cleaning protocols
  • Lack of verification testing

8. Regulatory Compliance

FDA Guidelines (United States)

Food Contact Surfaces

Title 21, Volume 3 - Code of Federal Regulations

  • 3D printed food-contact surfaces can be made compliant
  • Coating with approved resins is acceptable
  • Same standards apply as for resinous counters and tables
  • Materials must not impart taste, odor, or harmful substances to food

Requirements:

  • Materials must be suitable for intended use
  • Must withstand normal conditions of use
  • Must be cleanable and maintainable
  • Documentation of material safety required

Medical Devices

  • 3D printed medical devices require FDA approval
  • Must follow Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP)
  • Sterilization validation required
  • Biocompatibility testing mandatory
  • During COVID-19: Emergency Use Authorizations (EUA) waived some requirements

FDA-Approved Materials

  • PETG: FDA-approved for food contact (verify specific brands)
  • PLA: Generally Recognized As Safe (GRAS) but verify certification
  • Check manufacturer documentation for FDA compliance

European Standards

ISO 10993 - Biocompatibility

ISO 10993 Certification:

  • International standard for biological evaluation of medical devices
  • Required for medical device approval in EU
  • Tests for cytotoxicity, sensitization, irritation, and systemic toxicity
  • PETG (Taulman® guidel!ne®): ISO 10993 certified

CE Marking

  • Required for medical devices sold in European Economic Area
  • Indicates conformity with health, safety, and environmental protection standards
  • Applies to 3D printed medical devices

EU-Specific Regulations

  • Dry heat sterilization: Prohibited in EU hospitals (inactive against prions)
  • Food contact materials must comply with EU Regulation 1935/2004
  • Plastic materials must comply with EU Regulation 10/2011

Hospital and Medical Facility Requirements

Sterilization Protocols

Mandatory Requirements:

  • Sterilization is MANDATORY for surgical use
  • Must follow institutional sterilization protocols
  • Documentation of sterilization method required
  • Validation of sterilization effectiveness
  • Batch tracking and record keeping
  • Regular equipment calibration and maintenance

Point-of-Care Manufacturing

  • In-house 3D printing in hospitals requires quality management system
  • Design and manufacturing process documentation
  • Material traceability
  • Post-processing and sterilization validation
  • Risk assessment and management

Single-Use vs. Reusable Devices

  • Single-Use: Simpler regulatory path, no reprocessing validation needed
  • Reusable: Requires validation of cleaning and sterilization protocols
  • Consider application criticality when choosing approach

Food Service Establishment Requirements

FDA Food Code

Sanitization Requirements:

  • Food-contact surfaces must be sanitized
  • Minimum: 200ppm bleach water for 1 minute
  • Recommended: 2 minutes for best results
  • Alternative: 171°F hot water for 30 seconds (not suitable for 3D prints)
  • Quaternary ammonium compounds per manufacturer instructions

Local Health Department Regulations

  • May have additional requirements beyond FDA Food Code
  • Inspection and approval may be required
  • Documentation of cleaning protocols
  • Regular testing and verification
  • Consult local health department before commercial use

Documentation and Record Keeping

For Medical Use

  • Design files and specifications
  • Material certifications and lot numbers
  • Printing parameters and settings
  • Post-processing procedures
  • Sterilization records (date, method, cycle parameters)
  • Quality control testing results
  • Traceability to patient/procedure

For Food Service

  • Material safety data sheets (MSDS)
  • Food-safe certifications
  • Cleaning and sanitization logs
  • Testing and verification records
  • Maintenance and replacement schedules

Liability and Risk Management

Important Considerations:

  • Manufacturer liability applies to 3D printed products
  • Proper documentation protects against liability claims
  • Insurance coverage may be required for commercial use
  • Professional consultation recommended for commercial applications
  • Stay current with evolving regulations

9. Conclusion

Key Takeaway: Yes, 3D Printed Objects CAN Be Made Food Safe and Sterile

Based on comprehensive published research from multiple institutions, 3D printed objects can be made food safe and even sterile for medical use with proper:

  • Material selection
  • Printing parameters
  • Cleaning protocols
  • Sterilization methods

Critical Success Factors

1. Material Selection is Paramount

  • For Food Contact: PETG (FDA-approved) is the gold standard
  • For Medical Use: PETG or GreenTEC Pro® with appropriate sterilization
  • Avoid: ABS for any food or medical application

2. 100% Infill is Non-Negotiable

  • This is the single most important factor
  • Hollow objects cannot be reliably sanitized or sterilized
  • No exceptions for food or medical applications

3. Proper Cleaning Protocols Work

  • Layer lines are NOT the primary concern
  • Warm soapy water + baking soda + bleach water achieves 90%+ reduction
  • Properly cleaned 3D prints perform comparably to standard kitchen equipment
  • Verification testing is essential

4. Sterilization Technology Exists

  • Hydrogen peroxide gas plasma is the gold standard for PLA/PETG
  • GreenTEC Pro® uniquely withstands autoclave sterilization
  • Sub-millimeter deformations are clinically acceptable

Debunked Myths

Myth: Layer lines make 3D prints unsafe

Fact: Layer lines (1 micron average) are smaller than bacteria (2-5 microns). Cleaning solutions can penetrate 0.1 micron spaces. Layer lines are not the primary concern.

Myth: Lead from brass nozzles contaminates prints

Fact: Lead transfer is negligible (0.007g potential contact). No measurable loss after 1000 hours of printing. Brass keys expose users to 19× more lead.

Myth: 3D printed objects can't be sterilized

Fact: Hydrogen peroxide gas plasma achieves effective sterilization with <0.2mm deformation. GreenTEC Pro® can even withstand autoclave sterilization.

Myth: All 3D printed materials are the same

Fact: Material selection is critical. PETG is FDA-approved, ABS is not food-safe, and GreenTEC Pro® has unique autoclave resistance.

Practical Implementation

For Home/Hobbyist Use

  1. Use PETG or food-safe PLA
  2. Print with 100% infill (mandatory)
  3. Clean with soap + baking soda + bleach water
  4. Consider coating colored prints with food-safe resin
  5. Replace items regularly
  6. Use for low-risk applications

For Professional/Commercial Use

  1. Use certified materials (PETG with FDA approval)
  2. Implement documented cleaning protocols
  3. Use protein residue testing for verification
  4. Follow local health department regulations
  5. Maintain detailed records
  6. Consider professional liability insurance

For Medical/Surgical Use

  1. Use biocompatible materials (PETG, GreenTEC Pro®)
  2. Mandatory 100% infill
  3. Sterilize with hydrogen peroxide gas plasma
  4. Alternative: GreenTEC Pro® with autoclave
  5. Follow institutional protocols
  6. Document all processes
  7. Consider single-use for critical applications

Future Directions

Research Needs

  • Long-term effects of repeated sterilization cycles
  • Standardization of protocols across institutions
  • Broader pathogen testing panels
  • Comparison of additional material types
  • Effects of different printer brands and settings

Emerging Technologies

  • New biocompatible filaments in development
  • Antimicrobial filaments with embedded agents
  • Improved sterilization equipment for thermoplastics
  • Advanced coating technologies
  • Better regulatory frameworks

Final Recommendations

The Bottom Line:

  • 3D printed objects CAN be food safe and sterile
  • Success requires proper material selection, 100% infill, and appropriate protocols
  • Research-backed methods exist and are proven effective
  • Regulatory compliance is achievable
  • Professional applications should involve expert consultation

Most Important: Always use 100% infill and follow verified cleaning/sterilization protocols. This is not optional - it's essential for safety.
